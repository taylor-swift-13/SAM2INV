\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{Classical Loop Invariant Generation.}
Invariant synthesis methods span a spectrum from fully static to
fully dynamic.
Abstract interpretation~\cite{cousot1977abstract} computes
fixed-point over-approximations in abstract domains (intervals,
octagons, polyhedra) and underpins industrial analysers, but is
restricted to the expressiveness of the chosen domain.
Template-based constraint
solving~\cite{colon2003linear,sankaranarayanan2004non} fixes a
parametric invariant form and searches for satisfying coefficients
via LP or SDP; the approach is complete within the template family
but cannot discover structure outside it.
The Houdini algorithm~\cite{flanagan2001houdini} starts from a large
candidate set and iteratively removes non-inductive conjuncts; we
adopt it as the third stage of our filter pipeline.
Daikon~\cite{daikon} and DIG~\cite{nguyen2012using} mine likely
invariants from execution traces; like us they use traces as
evidence, but require a separate verifier pass to guarantee
inductiveness.
All these methods are confined to their pre-designed search spaces
and generalise poorly when the invariant falls outside.

\paragraph{LLM-Based Loop Invariant Generation.}
Recent work has applied LLMs to invariant synthesis.
Chakraborty and Lahiri~\cite{chakraborty2024towards} show that
GPT-4 can generate invariants for simple C programs with a
verification feedback loop.
Pei et al.~\cite{pei2023can} study code-trained LLMs producing
Hoare-style annotations and find that iterative refinement with
verifier feedback is essential.
Kamath et al.~\cite{kamath2023finding} propose a guess-and-check
framework combining LLM generation with bounded model checking.
In the proof-assistant ecosystem, LLMs suggest
tactics~\cite{han2021proof,jiang2022draft} and translate informal
statements into formal
proofs~\cite{wu2022autoformalization,lample2022hypertree,polu2022formal}.
A common thread across these works is heavy reliance on external
tool chains (SMT solvers, static analysers, multi-round repair
workflows) and the most powerful closed-source models available,
without exploring whether a small, locally deployed model can be
trained for this task.
\textsc{SAM2INV} differs in two respects: a systematic three-stage
filter pipeline that pre-screens candidates before formal
verification, and an offline distillation strategy that transfers
the capability of a teacher model to a small student without
requiring online exploration.

\paragraph{Knowledge Distillation vs.\ Reinforcement Learning for
Reasoning.}
Reinforcement learning from human
feedback~(RLHF)~\cite{ouyang2022training} and direct preference
optimisation~(DPO)~\cite{rafailov2023direct} are standard alignment
techniques for LLMs.
Replacing human preferences with verifiable rewards from automated
oracles---reinforcement learning with verifiable
rewards~(RLVR)---has produced large gains in mathematical reasoning:
DeepSeek-R1~\cite{deepseekr1} and
Tulu~3~\cite{lambert2024tulu} train on verifier-generated rewards
with no human annotation, while execution-guided
methods~\cite{le2022coderl,gehring2024rlef} use test-case signals
for code generation.
However, the DeepSeek-R1 distilled models~\cite{deepseekr1}
demonstrate that reasoning patterns of a strong teacher can be
transferred to small models via distillation more effectively than
through RL on the small model directly, because distillation
directly transfers successful solution trajectories rather than
requiring the student to rediscover them through sparse reward
signals.
In the loop invariant domain, the binary verifier reward is
particularly problematic: it is sparse (a weak model rarely
produces correct annotations), reward-hackable (trivial
annotations pass weak postconditions), and non-attributable (the
correct annotation is a conjunction assembled from multiple calls).
Our approach inherits the RLVR spirit but moves exploration
entirely offline into the data-preparation phase, using verifier
feedback to construct ground-truth preference pairs for SFT+DPO
distillation---avoiding the instability of online RL for weak base
models.

\paragraph{Training Data Synthesis for Program Verification.}
Existing invariant synthesis benchmarks---the NLA suite from
SV-COMP~\cite{svcomp}, the linear benchmark
set~\cite{si2018learning}---contain only a few dozen hand-crafted
programs, insufficient for both systematic evaluation and
large-scale training.
Grammar-based fuzzing~\cite{godefroid2008grammar} and probabilistic
program synthesis~\cite{nori2015efficient} generate programs at
scale but without the semantic constraints needed to ensure
well-defined invariant structure.
In the theorem-proving domain,
AlphaCode~\cite{li2022competition} applies large-scale sampling and
filtering for competitive programming, a paradigm related to ours,
but deductive verification with Frama-C/WP does not admit
step-by-step proof search---the verifier accepts or rejects a
complete annotation in one shot, making the filtering pipeline
critical.
\textsc{LoopFactory} addresses the data gap by generating structured
numeric loop programs with tunable complexity via semantic core
templates, supplying both training inputs and controlled benchmarks
for complexity ablation.
