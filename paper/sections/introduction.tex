\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%% ============================================================

Loop invariant generation is a central hard problem in program
verification.
Traditional
approaches~\cite{cousot1977abstract,colon2003linear,flanagan2001houdini,daikon,nguyen2012using}
can each handle specific families of loops but generalise poorly
beyond their pre-designed search spaces, and often yield limited
effectiveness at prohibitive analysis time.
The emergence of large language models~(LLMs) has brought fresh
perspectives to this problem, and several recent works have explored
LLM-based invariant
generation~\cite{chakraborty2024towards,pei2023can,kamath2023finding}.
However, these approaches typically rely on extensive SMT solving,
static analysis pipelines, and complex multi-round workflows, and
they universally employ the most powerful closed-source models
available at the time.
This reliance itself reveals that loop invariant generation remains
an exceptionally challenging task even for LLMs: it demands not only
\emph{logical reasoning} and \emph{program comprehension}, but also
mastery of the specific formal syntax and semantics of the target
annotation language.
Training a \emph{locally deployable} model that generates
high-quality loop invariants without relying on massive API token
budgets or heavyweight external tools---and at substantially lower
runtime cost---is therefore of considerable practical value.
Moreover, loop invariant generation differs fundamentally from
typical code generation or mathematical reasoning benchmarks: it
better reflects a model's integrated mathematical and programming
ability in a realistic formal-methods setting.
We therefore also aim to investigate whether small, open-weight
models can achieve strong performance on this task.

Before training can begin, we must first overcome the scarcity of
formal training data.
Existing invariant benchmarks contain only a few dozen
programs~\cite{svcomp}, which is far too few for fine-tuning and
raises severe data-leakage concerns.
To address both issues, we design \textsc{LoopFactory}, a
probabilistic domain-specific language~(DSL) constrained by semantic
templates, which synthesises well-formed numeric loop programs at
arbitrary scale (Section~\ref{sec:loop-factory}).

For loop invariant correctness we do possess a verifiable feedback
signal: an SMT solver can decide whether a candidate satisfies the
inductiveness conditions.
However, this signal is a binary pass/fail verdict---too sparse to
serve directly as a reinforcement learning reward.
More concretely, relying solely on the binary signal is susceptible
to \emph{reward hacking}: trivially weak annotations
(e.g.\ \lstinline|loop invariant true;|) can formally pass the
checker, earning full reward while capturing no mathematical
structure.
Designing a richer reward through hand-crafted heuristics or a
trained reward model introduces excessive \emph{human-prior bias}.
Furthermore, methods such as GRPO~\cite{deepseekr1} require the
model to roll out multiple candidate answers per input and update
the policy based on relative rewards; loop invariant generation is
hard enough that a weak base model rarely produces a satisfactory
answer within a limited sampling budget, rendering online exploration
ineffective.

Based on these considerations, we adopt a \emph{knowledge
distillation} approach.
As demonstrated by the DeepSeek-R1 distilled
models~\cite{deepseekr1}, reasoning patterns of a strong teacher
can be transferred to small models via distillation more effectively
than through RL on the small model directly; we believe distillation
better enables a small model to expand its reasoning repertoire and
explore novel solution paths for this task.
For the teacher we choose a relatively lightweight model
(GPT-5-nano), avoiding an excessive student--teacher capability gap
while keeping token cost tractable.
We move GRPO-style exploration into the \emph{data-preparation}
phase rather than distilling the teacher's single-best answer
directly.
In this process we embed the verifier's feedback signal: all
candidate answers sampled from the teacher are combined,
deduplicated, and filtered through a three-stage verification
pipeline; the surviving certified annotation is assembled as the
\emph{chosen} example, while every rejected candidate becomes a
\emph{rejected} example.
The student is then trained in two stages---first supervised
fine-tuning~(SFT) on the chosen examples, then direct preference
optimisation~(DPO) on the chosen/rejected pairs---transferring the
teacher's exploration benefit to the student, grounded entirely in
formal verification, with no human annotation or online reward model.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
In summary, this paper makes three contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{\textsc{LoopFactory}}, a probabilistic DSL for
  numerical loop synthesis that overcomes training-data scarcity and
  data leakage by generating programs with tunable complexity and
  known semantic structure
  (Section~\ref{sec:loop-factory}).
\item \textbf{A three-stage sampling-and-filtering pipeline} that
  issues $N$ parallel LLM calls, unions all candidates, and filters
  them through an ACSL syntax filter, an execution-trace semantic
  filter, and Houdini formal pruning via Frama-C/WP---certifying
  every surviving invariant
  (Section~\ref{sec:method}).
\item \textbf{An offline knowledge-distillation strategy} that
  embeds verifier feedback into the data-preparation step,
  producing SFT and DPO training data from teacher-model
  exploration, and fine-tunes a small locally deployable student
  model without online RL
  (Section~\ref{sec:offline}).
\end{enumerate}
